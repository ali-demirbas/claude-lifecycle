# English — Locale Overlay

The sector lexicon decides what to say; this file decides how English says it. English copy is written natively — never carried over from another language's sentence logic.

## Voice & register

- Contractions on by default ("you're", "it's"); their absence reads stiff, not formal. Enterprise B2B and finance may drop them deliberately.
- Direct address and short declaratives read confident; hedging ("we just wanted to reach out") reads like filler and gets cut.
- Verb-first CTAs, no politeness padding: "Finish checkout", not "Please complete your order".

## Emotion calibration

- **Reassuring:** built from stated policy and plain mechanics ("30-day returns, no questions") — adjectives ("reliable, premium service") carry nothing.
- **Warm:** first-name + the user's own data; endearments ("hey friend!") read as over-familiar in most brands.
- **Urgent (when allowed):** English tolerates mild urgency better than many languages, which is exactly why the data-backing rule matters more — "ends tonight" without a real date variable is the classic spam pattern.
- **Celebratory:** exclamation tolerance is higher than in TR/DE but still max one; "You did it — order #10" style restraint outperforms confetti language.

## Punctuation & typography

- Sentence case for subjects and headings; Title Case reads like 2010s growth-hacking.
- Thousands separator comma, decimal point (1,250.50); currency symbol before the amount ($12.50, £9).
- Dates: locale-split (US "July 12" / UK "12 July") — follow the audience market, not the brand HQ.
- Serial comma: pick one convention per brand and keep it.

## Market red lines

- US (CAN-SPAM/FTC): unsubstantiated superiority claims ("best on the market") and fake urgency are FTC territory; testimonials/results need substantiation.
- UK/EU (GDPR + ASA/CAP): "free" with hidden conditions, pressure tactics, and pre-ticked-consent framing are regulator flashpoints; comparative claims need evidence on file.
- Sweepstakes/"you've won" framing is restricted in most English-language markets — banned in marketing copy here regardless of sector.
- Health/earnings claims ("doubles your revenue", "melts fat") require substantiation everywhere English is a primary market; unverifiable versions get the legal-review flag.

## Character set

Standard Latin; preserve diacritics in proper nouns and loanwords (café, São Paulo, Müller) — folding a customer's name loses the personalization it was meant to carry.
